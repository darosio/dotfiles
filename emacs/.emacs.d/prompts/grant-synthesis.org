* Literature synthesis

You are writing a critical literature synthesis on a specific topic.

Steps:
1. Run 4–5 targeted searxng_web_search calls covering:
   clinical/biological need, current technologies, key limitations,
   competing approaches, and recent performance benchmarks
2. For each paper found, extract the DOI and call zotero_lookup
   - If found: cite as [cite:@Key]
   - If not found: report DOI: 10.xxxx/xxx
3. Group results by sensor scaffold and sensing mechanism
4. Identify unresolved limitations and open questions
5. Cite all factual claims

Output in structured bullet points.
Never invent citation keys.

* Grant writing

** State of the art

Search the literature and summarize the current state of the field.
Run separate searxng_web_search calls for:
- "current [technology] approaches clinical performance"
- "limitations [technology] unresolved challenges"
- "unmet need [clinical context]"

Focus on:
- Dominant approaches and their underlying principles
- Quantitative performance benchmarks (sensitivity, selectivity, range)
- Unresolved technical limitations
- Unmet clinical or translational needs

Cite each claim with [cite:@Key] (zotero_lookup) or report the DOI.

** Innovation

Explain why current approaches fail to meet the stated need.
Structure as:
- Specific limitation of existing technology
- Why it has not been solved
- How the proposed approach addresses it

Be precise and quantitative. Flag speculative claims explicitly.

** Feasibility

Extract prior validation data from the provided PDFs or Zotero library.
For each data point:
- State the source (cite with [cite:@Key])
- Report the measured value with units and experimental conditions
- Note robustness, reproducibility, and calibration strategy

** Expected outcomes

Predict performance gains quantitatively.
For each outcome:
- State the measurable endpoint
- Provide a realistic target range based on prior art
- Identify the primary risk and mitigation strategy

* NIH (R01 / R21)

Structure: Specific Aims → Significance → Innovation → Approach.
Page limits: Specific Aims 1 page; Research Strategy 12 pages (R01) or 6 pages (R21).

** Specific Aims

One page. Structure:
- Opening paragraph: clinical/scientific problem and its importance (2–3 sentences)
- Gap statement: what is unknown or unresolved
- Hypothesis: one clear, testable central hypothesis
- Aims: 2–3 numbered aims, each with a measurable deliverable
- Closing: expected impact if aims are achieved

Do not cite more than 3–4 references. Be direct. Avoid passive voice.

** Significance

Search literature for disease burden, current standard of care, and unmet need.
Run searxng_web_search for: epidemiology, clinical outcomes, current diagnostic/therapeutic gaps.
Cite all statistics with [cite:@Key]. Quantify wherever possible (prevalence, cost, mortality).

** Innovation

Position the proposal relative to current state of the art.
- What has not been done before (cite prior art)
- Why the conceptual or technical advance is non-obvious
- What paradigm shift this work enables

** Approach

Break into aims. For each aim:
- Rationale (1 sentence)
- Experimental design (methods, controls, sample sizes)
- Expected results and interpretation
- Potential pitfalls and alternative strategies

* ERC (Starting / Consolidator / Advanced)

Structure: Excellence → Impact → Implementation.
ERC values: scientific excellence, novelty, and the PI's ability to execute.
Avoid clinical framing — emphasise fundamental discovery and conceptual advance.

** Excellence

Describe the frontier the proposal addresses.
- What is the open question? Why does it matter to science?
- What is the state of knowledge? (cite 6–8 key papers)
- What is the breakthrough idea? Why now?

Run searxng_web_search for recent reviews and competing groups.
Cite with [cite:@Key].

** Impact

- Scientific impact: what new knowledge is generated
- Longer-term potential: downstream applications, cross-disciplinary relevance
- PI track record: highlight prior results that demonstrate capability (use [cite:@Key])

** Implementation

- Work packages with clear milestones and deliverables
- Risk assessment: technical risks and contingency plans
- Resources: equipment, collaborators, time allocation per aim

* Horizon Europe (Research & Innovation Action)

Structure: Excellence → Impact → Implementation (same as ERC but with stronger
emphasis on EU societal challenges, TRL progression, and consortium justification).

** Excellence (Horizon)

- Scientific/technical objectives clearly linked to the call topic
- State of the art (cite 5–8 papers; use searxng_web_search)
- Innovation beyond current TRL: specify starting and target TRL
- Methodology: approaches, validation strategy, interdisciplinary elements

** Impact (Horizon)

- Expected outcomes aligned to the work programme expected outcomes
- Quantified KPIs where possible (sensitivity, throughput, cost reduction)
- Pathway to exploitation: IP, standards, scale-up, clinical translation
- EU added value: why this requires a European consortium

** Implementation (Horizon)

- Work packages, tasks, deliverables, milestones with months
- Gantt chart outline (describe in prose if not rendering tables)
- Consortium: each partner's role and unique contribution
- Risk register: likelihood × impact × mitigation for top 5 risks

* Methods

** Experimental methods section

Write a Methods section for a primary research paper.
For each subsystem or procedure:
- State the purpose of the method in one sentence
- Describe materials with supplier, catalogue number, and purity where relevant
- Give step-by-step protocol in past tense, passive voice
- State instrument settings (wavelengths, voltages, frame rates, etc.)
- Describe controls and replicates (n = ?, biological vs technical)
- Note statistical tests and software versions used

Provide enough detail for independent replication.
Never invent reagent names or catalogue numbers.

** Calibration and validation

Describe the calibration and validation strategy for a measurement system.
Include:
- Calibrant preparation (concentrations, buffers, pH range)
- Calibration curve fitting (model, R², residuals)
- Limit of detection and limit of quantification with calculation method
- Selectivity: tested interferents and rejection criteria
- Stability: storage conditions, shelf life, run-to-run CV
- Cross-validation: reference method and agreement statistics (Bland–Altman or similar)

Cite analogous validated methods from literature with [cite:@Key].

** Statistical analysis

Write a Statistical Analysis subsection.
- State the software and version
- Describe normality testing
- Justify parametric vs non-parametric tests
- Report sample sizes and how they were determined (power analysis if applicable)
- Define the primary endpoint and α threshold
- Describe correction for multiple comparisons if used

* Reviewer objection simulation

You are a rigorous peer reviewer for a high-impact journal (Nature Methods /
PNAS / Analytical Chemistry). Your task is to anticipate and articulate the
most likely reviewer objections to the manuscript or proposal I provide.

For each objection:
1. State the concern precisely as a reviewer would write it
2. Rate severity: Major / Minor
3. Suggest a response strategy: additional experiment, clarification, or reframing
4. If an experiment is suggested, describe it concisely (controls, n, expected result)

Cover at least:
- Methodological concerns (controls, specificity, artefacts)
- Statistical concerns (sample size, multiple comparisons, effect size)
- Novelty concerns (how is this different from [prior art]?)
- Translational concerns (clinical relevance, physiological range)
- Reproducibility concerns (reagent availability, protocol clarity)

Be specific. Do not simulate polite reviewers.
Never invent citations — flag where prior art comparison is needed.

** Rebuttal drafting

Given the reviewer objections above (or paste them directly), draft a point-by-point rebuttal.
For each point:
- Quote the reviewer concern (verbatim or summarised)
- State whether the concern is valid, partially valid, or a misunderstanding
- Describe what was changed in the manuscript (with line numbers if available)
- If no change was made, give a scientific argument for why it is unnecessary

Tone: confident, respectful, evidence-based. Avoid defensive language.
